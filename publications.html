  • Dallas,T. and J.M. Drake. 2014. Relative Importance of Environmental, Geographic, and Spatial Variables on Zooplankton Metacommunities. Ecosphere. 5:art104. Website PDF

    • Quick Summary

    What controls which species occur in a given community? We addressed this central question in community ecology by examining small aquatic crustaceans distributed over much of the northeast US. To do this, we broke zooplankton down into taxonomic subsets (e.g., well defined groups of zooplankton), as different groups may respond more strongly to different variable. We determined the relative impact of geographic distance, lake characteristics, and physical variables (e.g., lake shape or area) on local community composition. We found support that the environment trumps space when it comes to zooplankton community composition, suggesting that dispersal isn't a big limitation for zooplankton, but environmental tolerances determine what species can persist in a given environment.
  • Dallas,T. 2014. metacom: an R package for the analysis of metacommunity structure. Ecography. DOI:10.1111/j.1600-0587.2013.00695.x Website PDF

    • Quick Summary

    This is an open access software note for a package that helps with the analysis of metacommunity structure. Metacommunities, as defined here, refer to a series of local communities connected through dispersal (e.g., a series of ponds). Specifically, some think it is possible to classify metacommunities based on three statistics calculated on an interaction matrix (i.e., a method of depicting species distributions among sites, parasite distributions among hosts, etc.). This package enables users to calculate these three statistics, and to create some informative plots.
  • Dallas, T. , S. Presley. 2014. "Relative importance of host environment, transmission potential, and host phylogeny to the structure of parasite metacommunities" Oikos. 123(7):866-874. DOI:10.1111/oik.00707 Website PDF

    • Quick Summary

    Hosts harbor a diverse suite of parasites. What mechanisms underlie parasite species distributions? This paper attempted to discern the relative role of host evolutionary history, host physiological traits, and traits associated with the potential for parasite transmission (e.g., host diet breadth). Host traits explained a majority of the variation in parasite community structure (similar to the role of the environment seen previously in the Ecology and Evolution paper). Further, this represents a new way of examining parasite communities, and provides evidence that parasite assemblages may be structured across host species (i.e., parasite communities are deterministically formed according to host traits).
  • Dallas,T. & J. Drake 2013. Nitrate enrichment alters a Daphnia-microparasite interaction through multiple pathways. Ecology and Evolution. DOI: 10.1002/ece3.925 Website PDF

    • Quick Summary

    Nutrient pollution has great potential to alter aquatic ecosystems (think of an algal covered farm pond). While the effects of excess nutrients on algal growth and ecosystem stability are fairly well-known, there is no such general understanding for how nutrient pollution will influence host-parasite interactions. This is mainly because the pollution could affect host, parasite, or the interaction between them to different extents. Here, we examined the influence of nitrate (a common introduced pollutant thanks to man) on the host, pathogen, and their interaction separately, using a series of microcosm experiments of an aquatic crustacean ( Daphnia species) parasitized by a virulent fungal pathogen. Elevated nitrate increased pathogen transmission and infection intensity (i.e., number of pathogen spores contained within a host), but also decreased both host and environmental pathogen survival. Taken together, this study provides a mechanistic view of how nitrate is likely to alter epidemic dynamics in natural and impacted lakes.
  • Dallas, T. , S. Foré. 2013. Chemical attraction of Dermacentor variabilis ticks parasitic to Peromyscus leucopus based on host body mass and sex. Experimental and Applied Acarology. DOI: 10.1007/s10493-013-9690-x Website PDF

    • Quick Summary

    What makes certain hosts "better" to parasites? Lots of people believe that they are more or less susceptible to mosquito bites or tick bites, right? For part of my masters research, I wanted to know what host characteristics may lead to higher attack rates by parasites. To do this, I examined white-footed mice and a common tick species. I built (with some help) a glass tube with 3 ends; one where the ticks went in, and two arms containing odor from different mice. Ticks chose their ideal host based on odor alone. I found that male hosts and larger hosts were preferred over smaller or female hosts based on chemical cues.
  • Dallas, T. , S. Foré, & H.J. Kim. 2012. Modeling the influence of Peromyscus leucopus body mass, sex and habitat on immature Dermacentor variabilis burdens. Journal of Vector Ecology. 37(2) DOI: 10.1111/j.1948-7134.2012.00236.x Website PDF

    • Quick Summary

    Parasites are aggregated among host individuals. So much so, that parasitologists have applied a "rule" initially developed to quantify the horribly skewed distribution of land ownership in early 20th century Italy (the Pareto principle), finding that 20% of the population owned 80% of the land. Here, 20% of the white-footed mice hosts harbor 80% of the ticks. Why? We built regression models based on field data to determine what factors influenced the aggregated distribution of ticks on hosts, finding that heavier, male hosts, spending most of their time in the forest (instead of an old field) were the most burdened by ticks.
  • Kim, H.J., J.E. Cavanaugh, T. Dallas , & S. Foré. 2014. Model selection criteria for count data with overdispersion and its application to the host-parasite relationship. Environmental and Ecological Statistics DOI: 10.1007/s10651-013-0257-0 Website PDF

    • Quick Summary

    When we think of regression models, we think of standard linear regression. This doesn't work so well though when your data are count data (e.g., I have 10 mice, each with a discrete "count" of ticks found on them), or when data are really non-normal (a key assumption of most parametric tests). Here, people much smarter than me tested a series of information criteria, which relate different models trained on the same data to one another, to determine which model fits best. They then developed variations on a few classic information criteria, and test them on host-tick data collected as part of my masters research.
